\begin{abstract}
Educational videos are now central to online learning, but many platforms still provide limited support for captions, navigation, and access to visual context. This paper presents a prototype multimodal pipeline that generates reusable artifacts from source video: word-aligned captions, scene-indexed visual descriptions, and structured summaries. The player exposes accessibility-oriented controls, including keyboard-first interaction, ARIA semantics, configurable text presentation, and word-level highlighting; these are treated as design affordances rather than as evidence of user-level benefit. A bilingual corpus evaluation was conducted on 24 instructional videos (12 English, 12 Russian; 3.29 h total) using an ASR-only baseline (B0) and the full visual-description configuration (B1). B1 remained faster than real time for every video, with a mean RTF of 0.433 and a median RTF of 0.424, compared with a B0 mean RTF of 0.193. ASR confidence is reported only as an internal diagnostic (mean 0.942), while the 91.7\% 15-second scene coverage result is interpreted as temporal coverage of indexed scene anchors, not as a measure of description quality or accessibility impact. The results support prototype-stage engineering feasibility under resource constraints and define the validation work needed before stronger accessibility claims can be made.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
In higher education and professional development, video-centric learning has become routine, yet pre-recorded lectures and screen-captured lessons can still present accessibility barriers. Prior studies report that captions, playback control, and navigational structure can support video-based learning environments \cite{Gernsbacher2015,Mayer2020,Horlin2024}. This prototype therefore targets synchronized captions, scene-linked descriptions, and structured navigation within a single playback workflow.

Existing practice often treats these needs as separate product features. The proposed work instead frames artifact generation as an end-to-end pipeline problem: one processing flow should produce synchronized outputs that can be reused by the playback layer and downstream tools \cite{IngavelezGuerra2022}. This framing is relevant when compute limits, API quotas, and implementation complexity constrain deployment.

The research question is: Can a resource-constrained multimodal pipeline produce synchronized accessibility-oriented artifacts with stable timing behavior and practical processing latency across bilingual educational video content while using adaptive scene selection rather than a manually fixed scene budget?

This work makes four contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Implementation of a unified multimodal pipeline that generates word-aligned captions, scene-indexed visual descriptions, and structured summaries.
\item Development of adaptive scene indexing that increases temporal coverage of low-variance content without predefining a fixed scene budget.
\item Design of an on-demand scene audio-description mechanism with caching and player controls motivated by accessibility, cognitive-load, and neurodiversity literature, while leaving user-level validation to future work.
\item Benchmarking on a balanced and normalized set of 24 educational videos in English and Russian.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\subsection{Captions and Learning Accessibility}
Caption use is not limited to disabled learners; prior work reports benefits for attention, comprehension, and recall across diverse user groups \cite{Gernsbacher2015}. In educational environments, automated subtitles can be useful under realistic conditions, but caption quality also depends on temporal alignment and segmentation \cite{Malakul2023,Mirzaei2018}. WCAG and media-accessibility requirements further emphasize synchronized captions, descriptions, navigation, playback control, and configurable presentation \cite{WCAG21,W3CMediaReqs,W3CMediaAccessible}. \textbf{Gap:} these requirements are often implemented as isolated player features rather than as reusable artifacts generated in one processing pass.

\subsection{Cognitive Load and Neurodiversity in Video Learning}
Instructional video effectiveness depends on pacing, signaling, and learner control. Media-quality problems in online lectures, including pace and speech intelligibility, can affect learning \cite{Lange2020}. Cognitive load during educational video is context-dependent and difficult to represent with a single metric \cite{Kruger2016}; recent neurodiversity-focused studies also identify transcription errors and limited playback or navigation control as potential barriers \cite{LeCunff2024,Horlin2024}. \textbf{Gap:} this literature motivates control and structure as design requirements, but it does not by itself validate the present prototype for ADHD, dyslexia, or neurodivergent learners.

\subsection{ASR and Timestamping Foundations}
Modern ASR systems have achieved practical utility through deep-learning pipelines \cite{Malik2020} and large-scale weakly supervised models such as Whisper \cite{Radford2022}. WhisperX and CrisperWhisper target timestamp precision through forced alignment and attention-based refinement \cite{Bain2023,Wagner2024}. However, ASR confidence is not a substitute for reference-based quality measures such as WER or CER, which require manually verified transcripts \cite{Machacek2023}. \textbf{Gap:} timestamping advances are often presented separately from deployable pipelines that combine transcription, visual scene understanding, and interactive rendering.

\subsection{Visual Accessibility and Audio Description}
Audio description and scene description provide access to visual information that is not available from the primary audio track. Prior scene-description work shows that descriptions should be accurate, objective, succinct, and useful for the intended viewer task \cite{Natalie2021}. In this prototype, scene-linked descriptions are exposed as optional on-demand spoken output rather than pre-rendered into the source media. The present study evaluates systems integration and timing behavior; it does not evaluate professional audio-description quality or user benefit.

\section{System Design}
\subsection{Pipeline Architecture and Artifacts}
The proposed system uses a \emph{Hybrid Multimodal Architecture}, which calls a remote multimodal LLM for model-dependent visual description while using deterministic algorithms for high-throughput media processing. To improve performance on consumer-grade hardware, the acoustic (ASR) and visual (scene detection) branches execute concurrently. Given an input video, the system emits three artifact groups:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Subtitle artifacts} $A_{sub}$: word-level timestamps, segment timeline, and WebVTT export.
\item \textbf{Scene artifacts} $A_{scene}$: time-indexed visual scenes with text-aware natural-language descriptions.
\item \textbf{Structure artifacts} $A_{sum}$: summary points and chapter markers derived from scene descriptions.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Interaction Layer and Artifact Rendering}
For word-level highlighting, the prototype uses a midpoint-based word grouper that assigns each word to the segment containing its temporal midpoint. No adjacent word-timestamp overlaps were observed in the evaluated corpus, but this diagnostic is not assumed to generalize beyond it.

In this prototype, native HTML5 \texttt{<track>} elements were insufficient for the selected interaction requirements. The custom subtitle renderer supports granular word-by-word highlighting with current, previous, and upcoming states, together with user-customizable font and contrast settings.

Audio description is played through the on-demand mechanism in Algorithm~\ref{alg:ad}. The player selects the nearest indexed scene within a 30 s tolerance, returns cached audio when available, or synthesizes speech from the stored description and caches it for later requests. The 30 s tolerance is a guard against returning a temporally stale description when no nearby scene anchor exists.

\begin{algorithm}[!t]
\caption{On-Demand Audio Description Resolution}
\label{alg:ad}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require $T_{\text{req}}$ (current playback time in seconds)
\Require $I_{\text{scene}}$ (array of indexed scenes)
\Require $L_{\text{req}}$ (requested speech language)
\State $S_{\text{nearest}} \gets \text{select\_nearest}(I_{\text{scene}}, T_{\text{req}})$
\If{$|S_{\text{nearest}}.time - T_{\text{req}}| > 30.0$}
    \State \Return \text{Error (No scene nearby)}
\EndIf
\State $CacheKey \gets (S_{\text{nearest}}.id, L_{\text{req}})$
\If{\text{exists}($CacheKey$)}
    \State $Audio \gets \text{read\_cache}(CacheKey)$
\Else
    \If{\text{google\_tts\_available}()}
        \State $Audio \gets \text{synthesize\_preferred}(S_{\text{nearest}}.desc, L_{\text{req}})$
    \Else
        \State $Audio \gets \text{synthesize\_fallback}(S_{\text{nearest}}.desc, L_{\text{req}})$
    \EndIf
    \State $\text{write\_cache}(CacheKey, Audio)$
\EndIf
\State $\text{pause\_video}()$
\State $\text{play\_audio}(Audio)$
\State $\text{resume\_video}()$
\State \Return $Audio$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Methodology}
\subsection{Prototype Configuration}
Prototype implementation currently focuses on Russian and English language support and instantiates the architecture described in Fig.~\ref{fig:architecture} with the following components:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Transcribing:} the system uses a Whisper model with \texttt{int8\_float16} compute type \cite{Radford2022}. This setting was selected for runtime efficiency; reference transcription accuracy is not evaluated in this study.
\item \textbf{Visual Semantic Extraction:} Visual transitions are detected with PySceneDetect and a predetermined pixel-difference threshold \cite{PySceneDetect}. A hosted \texttt{gemini-2.5-flash} multimodal model \cite{GeminiTeam2025} then generates descriptions of visible text, layout, and scene structure for indexed scenes.
\item \textbf{Adaptive Scene Indexing:} Because heuristic detection can miss visually static content such as screencasts, low-variance cases fall back to \emph{Adaptive Uniform Sampling}. This fallback targets a temporal sampling density of about two descriptions per minute, not semantic description quality.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Technical Evaluation}
\subsection{Experimental Design and Corpus Benchmark}
We evaluated the pipeline using a \emph{Bilingual Multimodal Corpus Benchmark} containing 24 educational videos: 12 English and 12 Russian. The manifest duration is 11,856.82 s in total (197.61 min, 3.29 h), with 99.28 min of English and 98.33 min of Russian video. The corpus is balanced by language, duration bucket (short, medium, long), and content type (talking-head, slide-centric, screencast, and practical demo).

The corpus contains 8 videos per duration bucket and 6 videos per content type. By duration, it contains 21.18 min of short, 51.71 min of medium, and 124.73 min of long videos; by content type, it contains 49.71 min of talking-head, 45.77 min of slide-centric, 48.59 min of screencast, and 53.54 min of practical-demo material. Videos were assembled from mixed public and curated educational sources and normalized to 1280$\times$720 at 30 FPS with clean-audio labels. Source provenance is therefore a limitation of the current internal benchmark. Processing was conducted on a local workstation (AMD Ryzen 9 5900HX,
NVIDIA RTX 3050 Notebook, 16 GB of RAM) to establish a consumer-grade baseline.

\subsection{Formal Metrics}
The study reports engineering proxy metrics, not direct measures of learning or accessibility outcomes:
\begin{equation}
\text{low\_conf\_ratio} = \frac{w_{<0.5}}{w_{\text{all}}}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\text{coverage}_{15s} = \frac{s_{\text{covered}}}{s_{\text{video}}}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\text{RTF} = \frac{T_{\text{processing}}}{T_{\text{video}}}
\end{equation}
where RTF $< 1$ indicates offline preprocessing faster than the video duration.

For implementation, the denominators for RTF and coverage are derived from the same effective video span: the end time of the generated timeline. Thus, $T_{\text{processing}}$ includes offline preprocessing only, and RTF is interpreted as batch throughput rather than end-to-end playback latency. ASR confidence and low-confidence ratio are internal diagnostics only: because no manually verified reference transcripts were available for this benchmark, WER/CER are not reported and the confidence values should not be interpreted as transcription accuracy \cite{Machacek2023}.

The metric $\text{coverage}_{15s}$ is temporal scene-anchor coverage: the proportion of the evaluated video span within 15 s of an indexed scene timestamp. It does not measure whether the generated description is accurate, sufficient, or useful. The 15 s radius was selected as half of the 30 s uniform-grid reference used in the post-hoc ablation, so a uniform 30 s grid is expected to nearly saturate this metric and should be read only as a temporal-coverage reference.

Additional diagnostics characterize alignment stability and visual indexing behavior:
\begin{equation}
\text{overlap\_ratio} = \frac{n_{\text{overlap}}}{\max(w_{\text{all}}-1,1)}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\text{scene\_density} = \frac{n_{\text{scene}}}{T_{\text{video}}/60}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\text{tail\_uncovered} = \max(0, T_{\text{video}} - t_{\text{last\_scene}})
\end{equation}
where $n_{\text{overlap}}$ is the number of overlapping adjacent word timestamps, and $t_{\text{last\_scene}}$ is the timestamp of the final indexed scene.

\subsection{Automation and Reproducibility Protocol}
All runs are conducted via a single driver script, which reads the corpus manifest and processes every video consecutively in B0 and B1 configurations. For individual videos, the implementation uses a multithreaded pipeline to reduce runtime. The driver stores pipeline artifacts and computes summaries and metrics in machine-readable form, including baseline comparisons and a JSON report for further analysis. Tables and figures related to metrics are based on the 24-video corpus.

The driver rejects runs before table generation when it detects non-positive runtimes, missing rows, or out-of-range coverage values. Valid artifacts for a configuration are reused, supporting cache-aware evaluation. Hosted model calls can still vary with model updates and network conditions.

\subsection{Post-hoc Scene-Selection Ablation}
We conducted post-hoc scene-selection ablation on a frozen evaluation snapshot to compare adaptive indexing with fixed scene budgets. The same cached B1 timestamps were used without rerunning the pipeline. Three caps (10, 20, and 30 scenes) and two uniform time-grid references (60 s and 30 s) were evaluated with the same within-15 s temporal coverage metric. These comparisons are coverage-oriented diagnostics, not alternative semantic scene-detection methods.

\subsection{Qualitative Case Selection}
In addition to aggregate metrics, four cached B1 artifact sets were selected for qualitative inspection: the lowest-coverage case, a screencast reference, the densest practical demo, and a talking-head reference. These cases illustrate failure modes but do not validate semantic correctness or accessibility benefit.

The selected cases are presented in the results section to link aggregate metrics with specific failure modes and design implications.

\subsection{Interactive Latency and TTS}
Separating Text-to-Speech (TTS) from corpus preprocessing allows the system to prepare artifacts without adding network-bound speech-synthesis overhead. A preliminary Google Cloud Neural TTS spot-check on several sentence-level prompts suggested that network-bound synthesis latency dominates interactive response time, whereas serving a cached audio response avoids repeated synthesis work. These observations are indicative and exclude production network conditions; they illustrate the value of request-level caching rather than providing a rigorous latency benchmark.

\section{Discussion}
\subsection{Interpretation of Baseline Overhead}
The B0/B1 ratio should be interpreted as an ablation-style system analysis. B0 captures a minimum viable subtitling mechanism using Whisper ASR wrapped with utility code, while B1 adds visual transition indexing and visual context extraction. The overall ratio of 2.25 means that the full multimodal pipeline approximately doubles B0 processing time while remaining below RTF=1.0 in this corpus.

The duration distribution suggests an amortization effect: initialization and API-call overheads affect short videos more strongly, while longer videos distribute these fixed costs across more content.

\subsection{Deployment Implications}
The main deployment dependencies are hosted multimodal and TTS services. They simplify prototype implementation but introduce model-drift, rate-limit, cost, and network-latency risks. Replacing them with open-weight models would require a separate evaluation of runtime, quality, and hardware requirements.

\section{Threats to Validity}
\textbf{External validity:} the balanced 24-video corpus may not represent all real-world cases, including overlapping speech, severe noise, extreme accents, or other video genres.

\textbf{Construct validity:} reported metrics are engineering proxies. RTF measures offline throughput, ASR confidence is not WER/CER, and $\text{coverage}_{15s}$ measures temporal proximity to scene anchors rather than semantic description quality. The results therefore do not measure learning gains, cognitive-load reduction, or accessibility benefit.

\textbf{Internal and user validity:} the visual context extraction and TTS stages use hosted APIs, which introduces network, rate-limit, and model-drift dependencies. The prototype has also not been evaluated with disabled, DHH, blind or low-vision, ADHD, dyslexic, or neurodivergent learners. The interface features are motivated by prior accessibility and cognitive-load literature, but direct user benefit remains untested. We mitigate engineering risks through artifact persistence and stage-aware reporting.

\section{Conclusion}
This paper presented a resource-constrained multimodal pipeline for generating reusable educational-video artifacts: word-aligned captions, scene-indexed visual descriptions, and summary/chapter structures. The prototype integrates ASR, adaptive scene indexing, hosted multimodal description generation, and on-demand TTS into one workflow without modifying the source video. Its contribution is therefore best understood as engineering feasibility and artifact architecture, not as evidence of user-level accessibility impact.

The 24-video bilingual benchmark shows that the full B1 pipeline remained below RTF=1.0 for every evaluated video, with a mean RTF of 0.433 and median RTF of 0.424, compared with a B0 ASR-only mean RTF of 0.193. Adaptive scene indexing reached 91.7\% mean temporal coverage within 15 s of an indexed scene, while fixed scene caps reduced temporal coverage in the post-hoc ablation. These results indicate practical prototype-stage performance under the tested workstation, corpus, and hosted-service conditions.

Future work should extend the evaluation before stronger accessibility claims are made. ASR confidence should be supplemented with WER/CER or manually verified transcript samples; generated scene descriptions should be reviewed for accuracy, objectivity, sufficiency, clarity, and instructional relevance; and the player should be evaluated with relevant user groups or accessibility experts. A larger corpus with explicit source provenance and license metadata would further improve reproducibility and external validity.
